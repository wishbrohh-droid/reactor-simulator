NUCLEAR REACTOR — INFORMATION & SAFETY GUIDE

HOW NUCLEAR REACTORS WORK

A nuclear reactor generates heat through nuclear fission — the splitting of heavy atoms (typically Uranium-235). When a neutron strikes a U-235 nucleus, it splits and releases energy as heat, plus 2–3 more neutrons that trigger a chain reaction.

This heat converts water into steam, which spins a turbine connected to a generator — producing electricity the same way as a coal plant, but without burning fuel.

The reaction rate is controlled by control rods made of neutron-absorbing materials (boron, hafnium). Inserting rods slows the reaction; withdrawing them accelerates it.

CONTROL SYSTEMS EXPLAINED

  • Reactor Power Slider: Sets the target power level of the reactor core. Higher power = more fission = more heat = more electricity.
  • Control Rod Slider: Controls neutron absorption. At 100% inserted, the reaction stops. At 0% withdrawn, maximum power is possible.
  • Coolant Flow: Water circulates through the reactor to transfer heat to the steam generator. Low coolant = overheating risk.
  • Emergency SCRAM: Instantly inserts all control rods to halt the chain reaction — the fastest way to stop a reactor.

SAFETY SYSTEMS

  • SCRAM System: Automatic shutdown triggered by extreme conditions. Inserts all control rods in under 2 seconds.
  • Emergency Core Cooling (ECCS): Floods the core with water if temperature becomes critical.
  • Pressure Relief Valves: Automatically vent steam to prevent pressure vessel rupture.
  • Containment Building: A reinforced concrete dome designed to contain any release of radioactive material.
  • Defense in Depth: Multiple redundant barriers between the fuel and the environment.

DANGER ZONES & READINGS

  • Temperature >550°C: Risk of fuel rod damage. SCRAM recommended.
  • Pressure >200 bar: Critical. Relief valves should activate.
  • Pressure <100 bar: Low pressure means inadequate coolant flow.
  • Radiation >500 mSv/h: Significant containment issue — evacuate area.
  • Power <10% with full withdrawal: Reactor approaching subcriticality.

NUCLEAR POWER FACTS

Nuclear power provides about 10% of global electricity with the lowest lifecycle carbon emissions of any energy source, including solar and wind.

Modern reactors have passive safety systems that shut down without external power or human action, using gravity and natural convection.

The fuel in a reactor is only ~3–5% enriched Uranium — far too low to make a bomb. A nuclear reactor cannot explode like a nuclear weapon.

One kilogram of Uranium fuel produces as much energy as 3,000 tonnes of coal.

SIMULATOR GUIDE

  • Start by raising Reactor Power to ~50–70%
  • Withdraw Control Rods to ~30% for balanced operation
  • Keep Coolant Pump active at all times
  • Watch temperature — keep below 550°C for safe operation
  • Hit SCRAM if any critical warning appears
  • Try raising power to 100% and see the warnings activate!
